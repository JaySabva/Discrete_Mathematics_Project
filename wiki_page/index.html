Needs of Cryptography :

   In our day-to-day lives, the use of cryptography is everywhere. For example, we use it to securely send passwords over vast networks for online purchases. Cryptography is the process of securing information by changing it into a form that people can’t understand unless they know how it was encoded. The original information is known as plaintext, and the encoded version of the information is known as ciphertext. The calculation or code used to change plaintext into ciphertext is called an algorithm and the process is called encryption. The opposite of encryption is decryption—turning ciphertext back into plaintext, or another readable form.
   Cryptography is an essential way of preventing that from happening. It secures information and communications using a set of rules that allows only those intended—and no one else—to receive the information to access and process it.

History of Cryptography :

   Cryptography, the use of codes and ciphers to protect secrets, began thousands of years ago. Until recent decades, it has been the story of what might be called classic cryptography — that is, of methods of encryption that use pen and paper, or perhaps simple mechanical aids. In the early 20th century, the invention of complex mechanical and electromechanical machines, such as the Enigma rotor machine, provided more sophisticated and efficient means of encryption; and the subsequent introduction of electronics and computing has allowed elaborate schemes of still greater complexity, most of which are entirely unsuited to pen and paper.
   The development of cryptography has been paralleled by the development of cryptanalysis — the "breaking" of codes and ciphers. The discovery and application, early on, of frequency analysis to the reading of encrypted communications has, on occasion, altered the course of history.

Mathematics of Elliptic curves on finite fields :

Elliptic Curves:

  Elliptic curves are curves described by the equation y2 = x3 + ax + b over an algebric field K . Where a,b ∈ K. It looks something like this:

Point at Infinity:

  The point at infinity is denoted by the symbol O. It is the identity element / point of elliptic curve maths.

O + O = O

P + O = P

Here, P + P + O = O

Point Negation:

  For Point P(x, y) on the Elliptic curve Q(x, -y) is defined as negation of P. Their sum results into the identity point.

Point Addition:

  For Points P and Q on the curve if the line passing from them intersects the curve at R then P + Q + R = 0. Here, S is negation of R ⇛ S + R = 0 ⇛ S = P + Q. Mathematically,

if m = (yq - yp)/(xq - xp) , then...

xs = m2 - xq - xp

ys = m(xp - xs) - yp

Point Doubling:

  If tangent at P intersect the curve at Q then according to the addition rule P + P + Q = 0 ⇛ 2P + Q = 0. If S is negation point of Q then, S = 2P. Mathematically,

Here, m = y'(x)(xp, yp) , then...

xs = m2 - 2xp

ys = m(xp - xs) - yp

Point Scalar Multiplication:

  We can multiply a point with a number k by using Doubling and Addition concepts. This algorithm generally takes log2k time to compute M = k*P.

If S= 2P, then...

3P = 2P + P = S + P

4P = 2S = S + S and so on...

Elliptic Curves over finite fields:

  For a prime number p, We can construct a finite field Zp with p number of elements. We can define a curve over Field Fp = Zp ⊗ Zp as y2 ≡ x3 + ax + b (mod p). Here, a,b ∈ Zp. The order of the curve n is the total number of finite field pairs that lie on this curve, including the point at infinity O.
  We can find cyclic subgroups of points such that if we add any two points or multiply a point by a number in a cyclic subgroup, the result will be within that same cyclic subgroup. There is no overlap between any two cyclic subgroups. If each cyclic subgroup contains r points, then the cofactor of the curve is h = n/r. It is better to have less cofactor for security efficiency ideally h should be 1 ⇛ All points on the curve should be in a single cyclic group.
